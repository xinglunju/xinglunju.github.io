Winds, Jets, and Outflows

 

There are three important concepts when we try to understand the process of star formation: winds, jets, and bipolar outflows. They are related closely to each other, sometimes as causes and effects, and debates about their origins, natures and influences are still with us.


Winds


Wind is a universally used concept in astronomy, ranging from coronal winds from our Sun, to galactic winds driven by supernova explosions. In star formation, the wind is important because it is proposed to drive molecular outflows. It is different from the ‘normal’ stellar winds from the protostars: although it can have contributions from the stellar winds, most of it is driven magnetocentrifugally from open magnetic field lines anchored on rapidly rotating circumstellar disks. There are two scenarios about how it works: the disk winds model and the X-winds model.

Outflows


Outflows are naturally created when either winds or jets launch the out-going material and accelerate entrained gas to velocities greater than those of the cloud.
